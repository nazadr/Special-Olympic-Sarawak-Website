About Special Olympics

What is Special Olympics?

Special Olympics is a global movement that unleashes the human spirit through the transformative power and joy of sports, every day around the world. We empower people with intellectual disabilities to become accepted and valued members of their communities, which leads to a more respectful and inclusive society for all.

Our Mission

Special Olympics provides year-round sports training and athletic competition in a variety of Olympic-type sports for children and adults with intellectual disabilities. This gives them continuing opportunities to develop physical fitness, demonstrate courage, experience joy and participate in a sharing of gifts, skills and friendship with their families, other Special Olympics athletes and the community.

Who Leads Special Olympics?

Special Olympics is led by a dedicated team of professionals and volunteers worldwide. The organization was founded by Eunice Kennedy Shriver in 1968 and continues to be guided by her vision. Today, it's governed by an international Board of Directors and supported by a global network of staff, coaches, volunteers, and partners who work tirelessly to promote inclusion through sports.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Special Olympics?

Special Olympics is a global movement that unleashes the human spirit through the transformative power and joy of sports, every day around the world. We empower people with intellectual disabilities to become accepted and valued members of their communities, which leads to a more respectful and inclusive society for all.

How many people does Special Olympics serve?

Special Olympics serves more than 5 million athletes with intellectual disabilities in over 200 programs worldwide. Through sports, health, education, and community building, we're creating a new world of inclusion and community where every single person is accepted and welcomed, regardless of ability or disability.

What is Special Olympics' mission?

The mission of Special Olympics is to provide year-round sports training and athletic competition in a variety of Olympic-type sports for children and adults with intellectual disabilities, giving them continuing opportunities to develop physical fitness, demonstrate courage, experience joy and participate in a sharing of gifts, skills and friendship with their families, other Special Olympics athletes and the community.

What impact does Special Olympics have on athletes?

Special Olympics transforms athletes' lives through the power of sport. Athletes gain confidence, improve physical fitness, develop social skills, and experience the joy of competition. Beyond sports, Special Olympics offers health screenings, leadership opportunities, and a global community where athletes can thrive. Research shows that participation leads to improved employment prospects, better social connections, and increased independence.

Who leads Special Olympics?

Special Olympics is led by a dedicated team of professionals and volunteers worldwide. The organization was founded by Eunice Kennedy Shriver in 1968 and continues to be guided by her vision. Today, it's governed by an international Board of Directors and supported by a global network of staff, coaches, volunteers, and partners who work tirelessly to promote inclusion through sports.

Who is eligible to participate in Special Olympics?

To be eligible to participate in Special Olympics, athletes must be at least 8 years old and identified by an agency or professional as having an intellectual disability, cognitive delay, or a closely related developmental disability. There is no maximum age limit for participation. Athletes of all ability levels are welcome, and Special Olympics provides divisions to ensure fair competition for everyone.
